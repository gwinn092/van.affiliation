## The quiet-hours stability test (the question that matters)

**Can you reliably refill in the windows you actually have — without turning every evening into a scramble?**

Quiet-hours stability usually depends on three things:
1) **Predictable refill windows** (you know you’ll get them)
2) **Fast enough AC recovery** (you can “erase drift” in that window)
3) **A calm fallback plan** (when you *don’t* get the window)

If you’re currently in drift, pause shopping and do the short diagnosis first:
- **[Van battery drift diagnosis](/van-battery-drift-diagnosis/)**

## What “quiet-hours friendly” actually means (recovery-first)

### 1) Predictable beats “peak fastest”
In quiet-hour scenarios, “best” isn’t the unit with the highest marketing number. It’s the unit that:
- refills reliably in the outlet windows you actually get
- doesn’t require perfect conditions to hit its claimed speed
- makes your routine repeatable (less stress)

### 2) Your refill window plan matters more than your battery size
Most quiet-hours recovery is some mix of:
- **Shore power** windows: **[Shore Power Refill Window Plan](/shore-power-refill-window-plan/)** (high-level planning)
- **Drive-time** windows: **[Drive Recovery Window Plan](/drive-recovery-window-plan/)** (high-level planning)

This page stays high-level on purpose: no electrical setup procedures. The goal is to help you choose a unit that fits your constraints and routine.

### 3) The 700Wh+ floor (why we keep it)
Below ~700Wh, quiet-hours living becomes a constant “charge chase,” especially in:
- heat waves (fans run longer)
- storms (solar doesn’t show up)
- rule-heavy campgrounds (fewer recovery chances)

If you’re under that floor, use: **[Under-$1000 Upgrade Plan](/under-1000-upgrade-plan/)**.
