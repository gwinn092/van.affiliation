Three times in 8 years I have picked up my power station, carried it into a coffee shop, and plugged it into the wall.

You cannot do that with a hardwired lithium system.

That's the whole argument, really. But let me give you the three situations, because they're the kind of situations that don't show up in gear guides — they show up in real van life.

## Situation 1: Three Cloudy Days in Colorado

It was October. I was parked near Salida, working remotely, and three overcast days wiped out my solar harvest. I had 300W of panels on the roof — adequate for most of the year, genuinely tight when the sun doesn't show up.

By day three, I was at 18% on the Yeti 1400. I had client work due. I grabbed the station, drove to a coffee shop, and spent three hours at a table while it charged. I worked the whole time. I drove back to my spot with 82%.

A fixed system doesn't have this exit. Your battery is in the van. If the solar isn't enough and you're not near a hookup, you're running the engine to charge — which is noisy, fuel-burning, and conspicuous — or you're moving entirely. Picking up the station and taking it somewhere is a third option that quietly solves the problem most of the time.

## Situation 2: Hotel Parking Lot, Florida Summer

July in a black ProMaster is an extreme thermal environment. My fridge was running hard — averaging 20–25W an hour in that heat (Measured), which adds up fast. I was in a hotel parking lot with no hookup, waiting out a storm system that had been sitting over the coast for five days.

I'm not staying in the hotel. I'm just parked there. But the hotel lobby had outlets.

I walked the station in, explained I was a guest of someone in the hotel (I was), and spent four hours charging at a table near the entrance. Nobody said anything. I came back with a full station and enough runway to wait out the rest of the storm.

This is not a story about being clever. It's a story about having options. A fixed system would have left me with fewer of them.

## Situation 3: Electrical Issue, New York Winter

I had a wiring issue in the van that took a week to track down — something in the 12V system was pulling current in a way I couldn't immediately diagnose. Rather than run the station through potentially compromised wiring while I figured it out, I disconnected it entirely and charged it externally while I diagnosed the problem.

This is the underrated case for portability. When something goes wrong with your vehicle electrical system, a portable station is not part of that system. It's independent. You can isolate it, charge it separately, and keep your critical loads running while you fix the underlying problem.

A hardwired lithium install is integrated with your vehicle electrical. When something goes wrong in that system, the battery is in the middle of it.

## What a Fixed System Gets You

I'm not dismissing fixed systems. They make sense for specific use cases:

- Daily loads that consistently exceed 1,500–2,000Wh
- Setups where solar is the primary source and you want maximum panel input capacity
- Long-term stationary use where portability doesn't matter
- Builders who are certain their system will never need to move

If your use pattern genuinely demands what a fixed system offers, build one. But make sure it's your use pattern demanding it, not just the idea that a "real build" has hardwired lithium.

## Why I Stay Portable

I've been living in a van for 8 years. The primary thing I've learned is that flexibility has compounding value. The more constrained your setup is, the more each problem costs you. A system that can move, be temporarily disconnected, be charged anywhere with an outlet — that's a system that stays useful through conditions you didn't plan for.

The three situations above weren't disasters. They were minor inconveniences that got solved quickly because the station could be picked up. In a fixed system, each of those situations requires a different kind of problem-solving.

Eight years in, I'll take the portability.
