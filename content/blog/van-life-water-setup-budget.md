Water is the second most important system in a van build after power — and it is also the one most people overcomplicate on the first pass. The good news is that a functional water setup can be simple, cheap, and genuinely livable. The mistake is building for a permanent house standard before you know your actual habits.

## Three Tiers of Van Water Setup

### Tier 1: Jugs and Manual Pump ($0–60)

This is the simplest and most common starting point. A five-gallon water jug, a hand pump or gravity tap, and a small basin for washing covers the basics for most daily use.

**Daily capacity:** A five-gallon jug holds 640 ounces — roughly 40 glasses of water or about 3–4 days of drinking and light food prep for one person. For two people or anyone who cooks more than simple meals, plan on refilling every 2–3 days.

**Refill frequency:** High. This is the main tradeoff. Filling a five-gallon jug is simple — gas stations, campgrounds, water stations, and grocery stores all work — but it requires you to think about water before you run out, not after.

**What you can do:** Drinking water, food prep, basic dish rinsing, brushing teeth. Washing hands with a hand pump is workable. A camp shower over a basin is possible.

**What you cannot easily do:** Running water for dishes, a real spray rinse, anything that expects consistent water pressure. Cooking with significant water use (pasta, boiling vegetables) goes through a jug faster than you expect.

**Honest assessment:** This works well for weekenders, short-term van lifers, or anyone still learning their water habits before building a permanent system. The cost is zero beyond a jug, and refilling is a daily habit many full-timers sustain indefinitely.

### Tier 2: Small Tank and 12V Pump ($100–200)

A small plastic or stainless tank (10–25 gallons) plumbed to a 12V water pump and a simple faucet is the most common full-time setup for budget builds.

**Daily capacity:** A 20-gallon tank covers 3–7 days for one person depending on habits. For two people with light cooking, plan on refilling weekly.

**Refill frequency:** Manageable. Most campgrounds and RV fill stations will fill a tank through a port in the side of the van. Some people use a separate fill jug to top up in cities.

**What you can do:** Running water at the faucet, consistent pressure for dish rinsing, hand washing, and light cooking. If you add a small propane-heated or 12V-heated water tank, you get warm water for dishes.

**What you cannot easily do:** Long showers, laundry, or anything that requires large volumes of water quickly. Running water for cooking still draws down faster than most people expect until they calibrate their habits.

**Power draw:** A 12V water pump pulls 3–8A while running — usually only a few seconds per activation. It is a negligible load on any reasonable power setup.

**Cost range:** $80–150 for a basic tank and pump combo depending on tank size. Add $20–40 for fittings, tubing, and a simple faucet. This is a one-weekend install for most builders.

### Tier 3: Built-In System (Larger Tank + Hot Water) ($300–800+)

A larger built-in system — 30+ gallon tank, 12V or propane on-demand hot water heater, proper cabinetry plumbing — is the full residential experience in a van.

**Daily capacity:** A 40-gallon tank covers 7–14 days for most people. Some full-time van lifers go three weeks between fills with disciplined habits.

**Refill frequency:** Low. This is the main appeal.

**What you can do:** Real sink experience, warm showers (with a proper heater), more relaxed cooking workflow.

**What you cannot easily do:** Change your mind about the layout. Built-in systems are permanent investments. If your build priorities change — and they often do in the first year — a built-in plumbing system is expensive to modify.

**Honest assessment:** Unless you have already confirmed through experience that you need a larger built-in system, this tier is usually a premature investment. Most full-time van lifers start simple, learn their habits over several months, and then build more permanently from that data. A $150 Tier 2 setup that you use for six months teaches you exactly what to build permanently. A $600 Tier 3 system built on assumptions often gets partially redone anyway.

## Grey Water: The Honest Reality

Grey water is the water that drains from your sink after use. In most places, dumping grey water on the ground is either technically prohibited or at minimum frowned upon. The legal reality varies by location, but the practical reality is consistent: you need a grey water strategy.

**Simple solutions:**
- A five-gallon bucket under the sink, emptied at dump stations or into toilets.
- A direct line from the sink through the floor into a contained grey water tank (same as your fresh water volume, roughly).
- Minimizing grey water volume by washing dishes into a small basin and dumping that basin intentionally.

Composting toilets and black water are a separate discussion. For water specifically, the grey water volume you create roughly equals your fresh water consumption. Plan your grey water storage to match.

## Drinking Water and Filtration

Not all refill sources are equal for drinking water. Campground water is usually potable but tastes variable. City tap is usually fine. Unknown sources require treatment.

**Practical options:**
- A countertop Brita or similar filter for daily drinking — cost $20–40, replaceable filters every 2–4 months.
- A gravity filter like the Sawyer Squeeze or similar for when you are uncertain about source quality.
- Stocking a case of bottled water as backup is fine if you are not philosophically opposed to it.

The filter investment is small. The peace of mind is real.

## The Recommendation

Start simple. Tier 1 is a perfectly valid long-term setup for people who move frequently and do not mind daily water thinking. Tier 2 is the sweet spot for most full-time van lifers — enough convenience to make daily life comfortable without the permanence of a built-in system.

Upgrade only when your current setup consistently bothers you, not because a build forum says you should. The right water system is the one that fits the life you are actually living, not the one that looks most impressive in a build video.
