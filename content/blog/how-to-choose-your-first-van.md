The van you choose shapes everything downstream: how much you spend on the build, how comfortable the space feels, how your daily stealth and travel patterns work. Getting this decision right before you spend money on a vehicle saves a lot of expensive course-correction later.

This is not a ranking of the "best" van. There is no universal best van — there are tradeoffs, and the right tradeoffs depend on your actual priorities. What follows is a framework for thinking through those tradeoffs clearly.

## The Three Main Types

### Cargo Vans (Transit, ProMaster, Sprinter)

Cargo vans are the most common choice for full builds, and for good reason. They offer the most usable interior volume, the cleanest buildout canvas (no second-row seat bolts or windows to work around), and the most flexibility for sleeping platforms, storage, and kitchen layouts.

**Standing room reality:** A high-roof Transit, ProMaster, or Sprinter gives most people 6'2"–6'4" of standing clearance. That is a genuine quality-of-life upgrade on long stays. Standard-roof versions are around 5'9"–6'1" depending on your insulation and floor build. If you are taller than 6 feet, a high-roof version is worth paying more for.

**Stealth potential:** Medium. Cargo vans with blacked-out windows and no visible build details read as work vehicles in most contexts. High-roof vans are more visible but still less conspicuous than RVs.

**Fuel cost:** Varies by model. The Transit uses a gasoline EcoBoost or diesel engine; fuel economy runs 18–22 MPG highway on the gasoline version. The ProMaster and Sprinter are similar in range. These are not fuel-efficient vehicles. Budget accordingly.

**Common mechanical issues:** The Ford Transit EcoBoost has a well-documented coolant system weakness on early models (2015–2017). The Ram ProMaster has a high-mount engine and a front-wheel-drive layout that makes some repairs more involved but keeps the floor flat and low. The Mercedes Sprinter is mechanically robust but more expensive to service. None of these are dealbreakers — they are things to know before inspecting.

**Build complexity:** The highest of the three types. A cargo van from scratch requires insulation, a complete floor, walls, electrical, and furniture. That is three to six months of part-time work for most first-time builders, and $3,000–$10,000+ depending on how far you go. ### Minivans (Sienna, Grand Caravan, Odyssey)

Minivans are the underrated option for people who want a lower-cost, lower-commitment entry into van life.

**Standing room reality:** You are not standing in a minivan. Seated work height is around 4 feet from floor to ceiling, which means the whole interior is a lying-down or low-seated space. Some people make this work extremely well with clever storage and a permanent sleeping platform; others find it claustrophobic after a few weeks. Be honest about how much you care about standing room before buying one.

**Stealth potential:** High. A minivan with tinted windows looks like a family vehicle. Nobody is checking whether someone is sleeping in a Sienna.

**Fuel cost:** Minivans are meaningfully more fuel-efficient than cargo vans — most get 22–28 MPG highway. On a long trip, that difference adds up quickly.

**Common mechanical issues:** The Toyota Sienna is exceptionally reliable with a well-documented service history. The Honda Odyssey has a transmission that needs attention on some model years. The Dodge Grand Caravan is cheap to buy but more maintenance-intensive at higher mileage.

**Build complexity:** Low to moderate. Fold-flat seats simplify the sleeping platform. Electrical is usually simpler because the space is smaller. A functional minivan build can be done in a few weekends for $500–$1,500.

### Full-Size Passenger Vans (Express, E-Series)

The Chevy Express and Ford E-Series are the older, cheaper, simpler option — especially useful when budget is the primary constraint.

**Standing room reality:** Standard roof versions are similar to standard cargo vans — around 5'9"–6' depending on build. High-top conversions exist but are less common.

**Stealth potential:** Medium-low. A full-size passenger van with windows reads more like a camper or a converted vehicle to most observers. This matters more in some contexts than others.

**Fuel cost:** These are older platforms with older engines. Highway fuel economy runs 14–18 MPG. For high-mileage travel, this is a meaningful ongoing cost.

**Common mechanical issues:** Both platforms are well-understood and have massive parts availability. Mechanics everywhere can work on an Express or E-Series. Older models may have rust on frames and floors that requires careful inspection. The simplicity of the mechanical platform is a genuine advantage.

**Build complexity:** Moderate. Converting from passenger configuration means removing seats (often bolted in with multiple points) and addressing window locations that do not exist in cargo versions. The floor and walls will already be finished to some degree, which helps and complicates in equal measure.

## A Decision Framework

If your priority is **maximum interior space and a complete custom build**, lean toward a high-roof cargo van. You pay more for the vehicle and the build, but the finished result is more livable for long-term full-time use.

If your priority is **lower cost and simpler entry**, lean toward a minivan or older full-size passenger van. Both can support functional van life with a fraction of the build time and expense. You trade standing room and build flexibility for faster starts and lower financial risk.

If your priority is **stealth and urban parking**, a minivan or a standard-roof cargo van with blacked windows gives the most flexibility without calling attention.

If your priority is **mechanical simplicity and cheap repair**, an older Express or E-Series with a gasoline engine is the easiest vehicle to work on anywhere in the country.

There is no wrong answer here. There are only honest tradeoffs.

## Pre-Buy Inspection Priorities

Regardless of which type you choose, these are the things that matter most before handing over money:

**Rust:** Surface rust on body panels is cosmetic. Frame rust is a serious structural problem. Pay particular attention to the rocker panels, the underframe rails, and around wheel wells. Bring a flashlight and spend time underneath.

**Roof condition:** For a livable build, the roof needs to be sound. Look for soft spots, delamination on sprinter composite roofs, or previous water damage that was painted over.

**Mileage vs. age:** High mileage on a well-maintained vehicle is often better than low mileage on a neglected one. A 200,000-mile van with full service records is a known quantity. A 90,000-mile van with no history is a risk.

**Service history:** Ask for it. If there is none, price accordingly. Vans without service history sometimes have excellent mechanical condition; they also sometimes have delayed maintenance that becomes expensive quickly.

**Test drive:** Unusual vibrations, wandering steering, brake fade, and transmission hesitation all matter. Drive it on a highway at speed if possible.

A pre-purchase inspection from a trusted mechanic costs $100–$150 and is worth every dollar. If the seller will not allow an inspection, that is information about the sale.

## Before You Decide

The most important thing you can do before buying is spend time in the type of van you are considering. Rent a cargo van for a weekend and sleep in the back. Borrow a friend's minivan and try working from the seat configuration. Read accounts from people who have lived in the specific model you are considering — not just built it, but lived in it through seasons.

A van is a meaningful purchase. The decision you make here follows you for years. The framework above is meant to clarify tradeoffs, not rush you toward a choice. Take the time to match the vehicle to the life you are actually planning.
