## What It Can Run (Van-Life Reality)

Our baseline use profile is fridge + MaxxAir fans + daily devices first, then cooking as spikes.

### Typical baseline day
- Fridge cycling
- One or two fans depending on weather
- Phone/laptop/lighting/device charging

### Cooking days
- Occasional electric cooking bursts are manageable when recovery is planned.
- Daily high-draw electric cooking is different: that’s a system design decision, not just a power-station decision.

### Heat + cloudy stretch stress test
This is where recovery matters most. In hot/humid weather, fridge runtime rises. Add low-sun days and raw capacity disappears quickly if recharge is slow.

## Living With It in a Van

The Elite 200 V2 is compact for its class, but still a ~53 lb unit. Placement matters:
- Put it where cable runs are clean
- Keep airflow clear for thermal management
- Build around how you actually cook/work/sleep, not where it looks best on day one

The goal is workflow stability: fewer emergency charging decisions, fewer late-day power tradeoffs.

## Ports & Usability (Practical)

For van use, output mix and access matter as much as total output:
- Enough AC headroom for common van loads

- App/settings can be useful, but your system should still work with a simple, repeatable charging routine

## FAQ

### Is this overkill for a beginner?
Not necessarily. If your routine includes fridge + fan + work devices and real weather exposure, extra margin often feels better than constant power management.

### Is recharge speed really more important than watt-hours?
For many van lifers, yes. A unit that refills quickly can feel more stable than a larger unit that recovers slowly.

### Can this handle cooking?
It can handle occasional cooking spikes. Daily high-draw electric cooking still requires system-level planning and realistic recharge infrastructure.

### Is this a good first step before a full electrical build?
Often yes. Portable-first can reduce install risk and help you collect real usage data before committing to a fixed system.
