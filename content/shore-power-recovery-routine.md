# Shore Power Recovery Routine for Van Life: A Simple Plug-In Plan That Works

For many van lifers, shore power is the fastest stability reset available. It gives you predictable recovery when weather and routine become messy. A simple plug-in workflow can reduce power stress without turning every day into battery management.

**Shore power rule:** plug in before you’re behind.

## Why shore power is the recovery cheat code

Shore power is consistent. That consistency makes planning easier and prevents the slow spiral where weak refill days stack up.

From a recovery-first lens, the goal is not perfect battery status at all times. The goal is reliable rebuild windows that keep your next day stable.

## The shore-power routine (5 steps)

### Step 1: Decide your recovery target (baseline stability, not 100% perfection)
Leave with margin for baseline loads—not a fantasy of perfect conditions.

### Step 2: Plug in early (don’t wait until you’re behind)
The earlier you reset, the less you need emergency load trimming later.

### Step 3: Prioritize the right loads while charging (baseline first; avoid stacking surprises)
Keep baseline loads protected while charging and avoid adding optional high-demand usage just because you found an outlet.

Common “surprise stacks”:
- Cooking while charging when you’re already behind
- Running extra comfort loads “because you can”
- Layering AC-heavy usage on top of normal baseline loads

### Step 4: Use charging modes intentionally (quiet vs fast vs balanced—conceptual)
Choose mode based on context: faster turnaround when you need recovery, quieter behavior when comfort matters, balanced behavior when both matter.

### Step 5: Leave with margin (plan next 24–48h around recovery reality)
Before unplugging, decide your next recovery window and keep your routine aligned to it.

## What to charge first (baseline vs comfort)

Charge and protect in this order:
- **Baseline:** fridge, essential ventilation, essential device charging
- **Comfort:** optional extras and convenience loads

This keeps core stability intact even when recovery windows are short.

## Common shore-power mistakes

- Waiting until power stress is already high
- Treating every outlet as if it can handle anything
- Running messy cable routines that create avoidable risk
- Only charging when desperate instead of planning repeatable resets

Safety stays simple and high-level:
- Use appropriate cords and clean connections
- Avoid overloading circuits
- Use GFCI-protected outlets where available
- Follow manufacturer guidance for charging behavior

## How this fits with solar and driving recovery

For most people, stability comes from layering:
- Shore as anchor
- Solar as harvest
- Driving as recovery support

This reduces dependence on any single condition.

## If you only plug in once a week

If weekly plug-in is your main reset window, keep your routine conservative between sessions:
- Protect baseline loads daily
- Avoid surprise comfort stacking after weak-weather days
- Treat shore day as a planned recovery block, not a last-minute rescue
- Pair shore with driving/solar when possible to avoid mid-week stress

## FAQ

### Do I need a big system if I have shore power?
Not always. Shore access can make smaller systems more workable, but your routine and weather still decide how much margin you need.

### Should I charge to 100% every time?
Use manufacturer guidance and your routine. The main objective is stable recovery, not perfection metrics.

### Can I run my loads while charging?
Often yes, but keep priorities clear. Protect baseline loads first and avoid stacking optional loads just because you are plugged in.

### What if outlets are limited?
Use the time you do get strategically: recover baseline, plan the next window, and trim optional loads until then.

### Is shore power enough by itself?
Sometimes, but layered recovery is usually more stable when weather or travel patterns shift.

### What is the biggest shore-power mistake?
Waiting too long and treating recovery as an emergency instead of a routine.

### How does this relate to the 700Wh rule?
At CAVL, 700Wh is the minimum recommendation baseline for power stations. Shore routines can make that tier more viable when used intentionally.

### Where do I start if my system feels chaotic?
Start with baseline-vs-comfort load separation, then schedule predictable shore recovery windows.
