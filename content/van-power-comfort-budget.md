Most van power conflict isn’t about watts. It’s about **fairness**.

One person thinks something is “basic comfort.” The other hears “unplanned drain.” In heat, that gap gets bigger fast.

This page is a simple framework to agree on:
- what counts as **baseline** (protected)
- what counts as **optional comfort** (conditional)
- what gets paused first in **Recovery mode**

If you want the full system this plugs into, start here: **[Van Power Recovery Stack](/van-power-recovery-stack/)**.

---

## The Comfort Budget idea (simple on purpose)

A comfort budget is not a spreadsheet. It’s an agreement.

You decide *in calm conditions* what your rig protects when conditions get hard.

This works best when you already use:
- **[Daily Van Power Mode System](/daily-van-power-mode-system/)**
- **[Van Power Weekly Reset Routine](/van-power-weekly-reset-routine/)**

## Step 1: Define your “Baseline” (protected category)

Baseline = what keeps you safe, functional, and stable.

Baseline is personal and climate-dependent. In heat, baseline often expands.

Heat note:
- **[Heat-Wave Van Power Recovery](/heat-wave-van-power-recovery/)**

Write your baseline as **categories**, not devices:
- safety/visibility
- minimum communication for logistics/safety
- sleep protection needs (varies by climate/season)
- food safety routines (your reality, not someone else’s)

Couples rule: baseline must be agreed on together.
- **[Shared Van Power Rules for Couples](/shared-van-power-rules-for-couples/)**
- **[Shared Van Power Check-In Template](/shared-van-power-check-in-template/)**

---

## Step 2: Define “Optional Comfort” (conditional category)

Optional comfort is not “bad.” It’s just **conditional**.

Examples of optional comfort categories:
- convenience appliances and “nice to have” routines
- background entertainment
- extended “AC-first” workflows
- comfort upgrades that are great *when you can recover*

The condition is simple:
> Optional comfort happens after you’ve protected recovery.

This is where “stop leaks” discipline matters:

## Step 3: Define your “Recovery Mode pauses” (pause-first category)

This is the key line that prevents fights:
> “When we’re in Recovery mode, we pause these categories first.”

Use:
- **[Recovery Mode Load Priorities](/recovery-mode-load-priorities/)**

Keep it short. One to three categories is enough.

---

## Step 4: Tie comfort to mode (so you don’t renegotiate daily)

This is the whole point: reduce negotiations.

A simple default:
- **Normal:** baseline + optional comfort (with guardrails)
- **Conservation:** baseline + limited optional comfort
- **Recovery:** baseline only + pause-first categories + one recovery move

Mode system:
- **[Daily Van Power Mode System](/daily-van-power-mode-system/)**

## Step 5: Cooking is the most common comfort conflict (handle it explicitly)

Cooking decisions under hunger create the worst arguments.

Use a guide instead of debating in the moment:
- **[Cook or Conserve Power Decision Guide](/cook-or-conserve-power-decision-guide/)**

Comfort budget rule:
> “Cooking choices follow the guide when we’re not stable.”

## Step 6: Put the agreement somewhere you’ll actually use it

Don’t hide this in a long note. Make it reachable:
- add it to your check-in template
- print it near the power area
- keep a short version on both phones

## The 60-second comfort budget script (say it out loud)

> “Baseline stays. Optional comfort is conditional.  
> If we’re in Recovery mode, we pause our pause-first categories.  
> Then we choose one recovery move and reassess.”

## When you keep fighting about comfort (what it usually signals)

Recurring comfort fights usually mean one of these:
- your baseline needs aren’t being respected
- your recovery routine isn’t strong enough for your comfort expectations
- you’re leaking power through workflow choices
- your plan assumes “good days” too often
